Why these two exercises?

We looked at outcomes from every past Taste the Soup session. The pattern: exercises where leaders experience the product firsthand produce the most actionable findings. Individual deep-dive audits consistently uncovered more than group reviews of data.

Offers Secret Shop puts you in the customer's seat. You'll experience the Offers inquiry flow, hear an HC call, and see what automated communications fire and when. Previous sessions uncovered significant timing and messaging conflicts in this flow. We want to see where things stand now.

Portal Updates puts you in the agent's seat. Portal updates are a known friction point, and improving the update experience is something we'll be prioritizing. This is a chance for every leader to feel firsthand what the update process is actually like, so we're all informed when we start making changes.

Discussion Prompts

The texts say "Hi Sam ?" with a broken name, and "??:" before phone numbers. How many of these did a human ever look at?

Footnote

Takeaways from the first secret shop

The timeline above is the second secret shop. The first one (a different property) had its own takeaways worth keeping visible, even if they sit outside the main walkthrough.

  • ·The same broken "?" showed up in the drip texts where the customer's name should be. You can see it all over the messages above too ("Hi Sam ?").
  • ·I heard about investors on the HC call, but I never got matched with one. Dillon's HubSpot record shows zero investors connected. Drake and Mike confirmed one investor did pop for the address, but the home was over that investor's max home value (outside their buy box), so no match ever came through.
  • ·I got connected with two agents. One reached me by live transfer. The other never called and finally texted at 9:30 at night.
  • ·The live-transfer agent led with the numbers: what the home was worth, how much I owed, and basically that a cash offer would not work because I would not net enough to pay off what I owe. He did push for an appointment, but only after delivering that bad news, and I left the call feeling deflated. That is the opposite of Addie in the second shop, who crushed the call and led with getting an appointment so they could walk through everything together.

Why this matters

Next up
Making updates simple is a priority

We've made real progress on portal updates. More agents are engaging than ever before. But we know the next unlock is making the update process itself much simpler, especially for high-volume agents. Right now, submitting an update requires a lot of clicking through a slow process.

When agents don't update, everyone is working with less information: CX, the agent, and the customer. This exercise gives every leader the chance to experience exactly what that process feels like so we're all on the same page when we start making changes.

How this exercise works

1
Log into one of your group's test agent portals

Navigate to agents.listwithclever.com/portal using the provided credentials.

2
Find your assigned deals

Each person is assigned specific deals to update. Search for your assigned customer names in the portal. Your assignments are on the cheat sheet.

3
Enter the updates from your cheat sheet

Each deal has a realistic update scenario. Enter the update through the portal UI exactly as an agent would: pick the stage, write the notes, submit.

4
Document every friction point as you go

Use the friction log below. How many clicks? Confusing labels? Slow loads? Write it down while it's fresh.

Hands-On: Portal Updates

Log in, find your assigned deals, enter the updates. Document friction as you go.

Where to log in: your group has its own set of test agents. Each agent's portal link is on your cheat sheet: Group A or Group B.
